Your Workplace is a Replica

Institutional Isomorphism

October 10, 2025

McKinsey advised Enron, Swissair, and General Motors. Before their collapses. Also their competitors. Same frameworks. Same consultants.

Pattern

Jeffrey Pfeffer studied Silicon Valley. Every company claimed uniqueness while adopting:

  • 20-30% annual turnover ("flexibility")
  • Extensive contractors ("agility")
  • Stock options ("alignment")
  • Long hours ("commitment")

Data shows these practices destroy value. Massive recruitment costs. Knowledge hemorrhaging. Burnout epidemics.

We know. We continue.

Tom Peters documented 43 "excellent" companies with unique practices. Within five years, most struggled. But everyone had already copied their excellence.

Mechanism

DiMaggio and Powell (1983). Three forces create organizational sameness:

  • Coercive: Regulations. Standards. Customer demands. You implement SOC2 not for security but for enterprise sales. Quarterly earnings not for planning but for Wall Street.
  • Mimetic: Under uncertainty, copy. Nobody understands AI strategy. Everyone copies Big Tech.
  • Normative: Professional networks spread practices. MBA programs. Consulting firms. Conferences. LinkedIn. Same frameworks taught everywhere. Same people rotating between companies.

Meyer and Rowan (1977) revealed the logic: organizations adopt practices for legitimacy, not effectiveness.

Being seen as a "real" company matters more than being an effective one. A ten-person startup implements performance reviews. Everyone knows everyone's performance. But real companies have performance reviews. They adopt Scrum. Not because it fits their work. Modern tech companies use Agile.

Contagion

30,000 McKinsey consultants. 90% of Fortune 100 as clients.

Consultant learns something at Coca-Cola. Monday. Applies it at Pepsi. Tuesday. Moves to tech sector. Wednesday. Now tech companies have brand managers. Thursday.

Why would competitors share consultants?

Professional conferences. Vendors showing best practices. Everyone taking notes. Everyone implementing the same solutions to different problems.

The network effect scales exponentially.

Cargo

Organizations copy what they can see. Miss what matters.

Amazon's two-pizza teams. Without Amazon's ownership model. Spotify's squads. Without Spotify's engineering culture. Google's 20% time. Without Google's margins.

We adopt the artifacts. Miss the dynamics.

Remember the Four Frame Tournaments? Organizations copy the visible -- Structural (org charts) and Symbolic (cultural artifacts). Can't see the invisible -- Political (power dynamics) and Human Resources (actual relationships).

It's like photocopying a shadow and wondering why it doesn't cast light.

Evolution

Watch any startup: 5 people: "We're different." 20 people: Look around. What do 20-person companies do? 50 people: Hire from Big Tech. Import their practices. 100 people: Need real HR. They bring best practices. 150 people: Indistinguishable from every other 150-person company.

Performance reviews require calibration. Calibration requires levels. Levels require promotions. Promotions require tournaments. Tournaments require everything we discussed.

Each decision rational. The destination inevitable.

HR knows performance reviews fail. Engineering knows Agile is theater. Executives know strategies don't work.

Copy and fail: "We followed best practices." Protected. Innovate and fail: "Why didn't you follow standards?" Exposed.

The board doesn't ask about stack ranking's damage. They ask why you don't have performance management.

Investors don't question recruitment burn. They question why you're not "scaling."

These aren't failures. They're features. Selected for over decades.

Organizations that look legitimate get resources. Organizations that act different don't. Organizations that copy survive. Organizations that innovate may get selected against.

The isomorphism isn't a problem. It's the solution. To a different problem than you thought we were solving. And you have a role to play.
